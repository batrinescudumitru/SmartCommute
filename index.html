Methodology & Sources

Scientific basis

This simulator uses effective doorstep-to-doorstep speeds, not road speeds. They account for parking time, walking to/from vehicle, waiting at stops, and queue delays — the only metric that matters for a real commute.

Comparison: Budapest 17.2 km/h, Madrid 17.5, Warsaw 19.4, Prague 20.1, Istanbul 20.7, Stockholm 22.6 km/h (all rush-hour road speeds). Note: road speed ≠ door-to-door effective speed — add parking ~10 min + walking → 6–7 km/h effective for car in dense city. tomtom.com/traffic-index/bucharest ↗
  2. Numbeo Traffic Index — Bucharest & Eastern Europe Rankings, 2024–2025. Numbeo.com. Bucharest: Traffic Index 165.98, average one-way commute 41 min over 10.54 km (≈ 15.4 km/h road speed; door-to-door effective speed is lower). Comparison: Warsaw ~30 km/h, Budapest ~26 km/h, Prague ~32 km/h at rush hour (all road speed). numbeo.com/traffic/Bucharest ↗ · EE Rankings 2024 ↗
  3. INRIX Global Traffic Scorecard 2022–2024. INRIX Inc. Measured average congested car speeds: London 16–21 km/h, Paris 16–21 km/h, Munich 18–21 km/h, Istanbul 21–24 km/h, Palermo 14 km/h (closest benchmark to Bucharest). European drivers lose avg 55–101 hours/year to congestion depending on city. Methodology: GPS probe data, average speed across the city road network during congested periods. inrix.com/scorecard 2023 ↗ · 2024 ↗
  4. European Cyclists' Federation — Cycling Facts & Figures, 2022. Cycling is the fastest mode for urban trips under 8 km during peak hours; door-to-door speed 12–15 km/h in dense urban contexts. ecf.com ↗
  5. Twisk D. et al. — Speed characteristics of bicycles in naturalistic urban traffic. Accident Analysis & Prevention, 2021. DOI: 10.1016/j.aap.2020.105940. Conventional bicycles average 17.8 km/h in real urban conditions (Netherlands). E-bikes: 20.1 km/h. PubMed ↗
  6. Schantz P. et al. — Estimating duration-distance relations in cycle commuting. PLoS One, 2018. Stockholm commuters (n=455, ages 20–65): male average 20.76 km/h, female 16.14 km/h. PubMed ↗
  7. ACEM — Powered Two-Wheelers as a Contribution to Sustainable Urban Mobility, 2019. PTW commuters 2.5–4× faster than car in peak-hour urban traffic; lane-filtering reduces commute time by ~37%. acem.eu ↗
  8. Transport for London — Travel in London Report 12, 2019. Central London average car speed: 14 km/h; cycling: 20 km/h door-to-door. Referenced as Western European dense-city benchmark comparable to Bucharest conditions. tfl.gov.uk ↗
  9. UITP — Global Trolleybus Figures 2025. Statistics Brief, May 2025. Real measured commercial speeds for urban transit in EU cities: Sofia 18 km/h, Vilnius 18 km/h, Riga 15 km/h, Budapest 14.6 km/h, Milan 13 km/h (road speed). Bucharest: 269 trolleybuses, 3rd largest EU fleet. Door-to-door effective speed lower by ~30–40% due to walk and wait components. uitp.org ↗
  10. EEA — Transport and Environment Report 2021 (Report No 02/2022) + New Car Fleet CO₂ 2025. Well-to-wheel GHG per passenger-km (EU-27, 2018): car 143 g CO₂e, bus/coach 80 g CO₂e, train 33 g CO₂e. New car fleet 2025: 96.7 g CO₂/km (WLTP). Bicycle lifecycle: ~8 g CO₂e/km (CE Delft 2022). Active modes described as "close to zero emissions" per EEA. eea.europa.eu/TER2021 ↗
  11. WHO — Benefits of Active Travel for Health, 2022. 30 min/day cycling reduces all-cause mortality by 28% and cardiovascular risk by 35%. who.int ↗
  12. Ungvari Z. et al. — The multifaceted benefits of walking for healthy aging: from Blue Zones to molecular mechanisms. GeroScience, 2023. PMC10643563. Walking associated with 32% reduced all-cause mortality risk. 8,000–10,000 steps/day reduces risk of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and dementia. Brisk walking: 38–53% lower cardiovascular mortality. 30-min walk 5×/week = 19% reduction in coronary heart disease risk. PubMed Central ↗
  13. Lee I-M — Micro-walks can have big health benefits. Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, May 2026. Short walking bursts accumulate benefits equivalent to longer sessions. Fitness in middle age linked to 1–2 extra years of healthy life after 65. Regular walking functions as a "wonder drug" reducing chronic disease risk. Harvard Chan School ↗
  14. Walker M.P. — Why We Sleep. Scribner, 2017. Each additional hour of sleep per night associated with improvements in cognitive performance and longevity. Referenced for sleep savings calculation.
  15. Mogridge M.J.H. — Travel in Towns. Macmillan, 1990. Classic reference establishing speed parity between car and public transport in congested cities — the theoretical basis for dense-city effective speed convergence.
  16. Marchetti C. — Anthropological invariants in travel behavior. Technological Forecasting and Social Change, 47(1):75–88, 1994. DOI: 10.1016/0040-1625(94)90041-8. Marchetti's Constant: humans consistently spend ~1 hour/day total on commuting (~30 min one-way) regardless of era or transport mode. Based on Zahavi (1974) foundational work. As faster vehicles become available, people commute farther, not shorter — time savings from switching vehicles are real and persistent.
  17. Zahavi Y. — Traveltime Budgets and Mobility in Urban Areas. FHWA PL-8183, US Dept. of Transportation, 1974. Original empirical basis for the travel time budget constant: ~60 min/day total, ~30 min one-way. Confirmed cross-culturally. Mokhtarian & Chen (2004, Transportation Research Part A) describe it as a "soft attractor" with significant variation around the mean.
  18. Pucher J. & Buehler R. — Making Cycling Irresistible: Lessons from the Netherlands, Denmark and Germany. Transport Reviews, 28(4):495–528, 2008. Cycling door-to-door competitive with or faster than car for urban trips under 7–10 km across multiple European countries. CROW Design Manual (2007) gives urban utility cycling door-to-door speed as 14–18 km/h.
  19. Biró & Lukács — Driving a Car is Shockingly Slow. Clean Cities Campaign, January 2026. Budapest: nominal car speed 22.6 km/h, real effective speed (including cost-earning time) 8.2 km/h. Demonstrates large gap between road speed and true effective speed. cleancitiescampaign.org ↗
  20. Fietsersbond (Dutch Cyclists' Union) — cited in municipal data, Groningen, 2009. Door-to-door measurement: bicycle 14.2 km/h vs car 9.6 km/h in Groningen — bicycles 48% faster. Dutch cities average: cyclists 5% faster overall, 10%+ faster in cities >100,000 inhabitants. aviewfromthecyclepath.com ↗
  21. Meira L.H. et al. — Measuring social effective speed to improve sustainable mobility policies in developing countries. Transportation Research D, Vol. 78, 2020. DOI: 10.1016/j.trd.2019.12.002. Social effective speed (distance / total time including work hours to pay for transport): Bicycle 16.74 km/h · Walking 10.91 km/h · Motorcycle 8.88 km/h · Private Car 6.34 km/h · Public Transport 5.23 km/h. Car ranks 5th out of 6 modes in effective speed. doi.org ↗
  22. Ahmed A. & Stopher P. — Seventy Minutes Plus or Minus 10: A Review of Travel Time Budget Studies. Transport Reviews, 34(5):607–625, 2014. DOI: 10.1080/01441647.2014.946460. Meta-analysis confirming Marchetti's Constant: global average daily travel time budget = 70 minutes/day ± 10 minutes. Time savings from faster transport are typically consumed by travelling farther, not working or resting more.
  23. Illich I. — Energy and Equity. Harper & Row, 1974. Verified from primary source (ivanillich.org.mx): "El americano típico consagra más de 1 600 horas por año a su automóvil... Estas 1 600 horas le sirven para hacer unos 10 000 km de camino, o sea 6 km en una hora." The typical American's car effective speed = ~6 km/h — equivalent to walking pace — when all time spent earning money to pay for it is included. Bicycle's effective speed is higher because ownership costs are minimal. ivanillich.org.mx ↗

Advanced emissions calculator — data source:
The Advanced mode emission factors (NOx, CO, PM, VOC per km) are calculated using the official EMEP/EEA Air Pollutant Emission Inventory Guidebook 2024, published by the European Environment Agency. The polynomial formula EF(V) = (α·V² + β·V + γ + δ/V) / (ε·V² + ζ·V + η) is applied at the effective door-to-door speed for each road type. 560 combinations extracted: 4 body types × 7 fuel types × 5 Euro standards × 4 road types.
EEA EMEP/EEA Guidebook 2024 ↗
